=====================================================================
  CHERNOBYL — APRIL 26, 1986
  RBMK-1000 Unit 4 — The Worst Nuclear Accident in History
=====================================================================

This document describes the historical events behind the Chernobyl
scenario included with this simulation, sourced from the IAEA
INSAG-7 report, the World Nuclear Association, and the OECD Nuclear
Energy Agency. All timestamps and technical details are drawn from
these primary references.


=====================================================================
  THE REACTOR: RBMK-1000
=====================================================================

The RBMK-1000 (Reaktor Bolshoy Moshchnosti Kanalnyy — "High-Power
Channel Reactor") was a Soviet-designed graphite-moderated,
light-water-cooled reactor producing 3,200 MWt / 1,000 MWe.

Key design characteristics:

  Moderator:     Graphite (1,700 tonnes)
  Coolant:       Light water (in 1,661 individual pressure tubes)
  Fuel:          2% enriched UO2 (fresh; ~1.3% effective at accident)
  Control rods:  211 boron carbide rods with graphite displacers
  Rod speed:     ~0.4 m/s (18-21 seconds for full 7m insertion)

Two fatal design flaws made Chernobyl possible:

  1. POSITIVE VOID COEFFICIENT
     In most Western reactors (PWR/BWR), water is both coolant and
     moderator. If water boils away, moderation stops and the
     reaction slows. In the RBMK, graphite is the moderator.
     Water primarily absorbs neutrons. If it turns to steam,
     absorption decreases and reactivity INCREASES — a positive
     feedback loop.

  2. GRAPHITE DISPLACER TIPS ("Positive Scram" Effect)
     Each control rod consists of a boron carbide absorber section
     connected by a telescope to a 4.5-meter graphite displacer.
     When fully withdrawn, the displacer sits centrally in the
     fuelled region with 1.25 meters of water at each end.

     When the rod is inserted from fully withdrawn, the graphite
     tip enters the lower core FIRST, displacing neutron-absorbing
     water with neutron-moderating graphite. For the first 11-14
     seconds of rod insertion, the lower core experiences a local
     INCREASE in reactivity — the opposite of what an emergency
     shutdown should do.

     This effect was discovered experimentally in 1983 during
     startup of Ignalina-1 (Lithuania) and Chernobyl-4 itself.
     The scientific manager described it as "extremely dangerous."
     Proposals to redesign the rods were made but never implemented.

     INSAG-7 later stated:
     "Apparently, there was a widespread view that the conditions
     under which the positive scram effect would be important would
     never occur."

     They occurred in almost every detail on the night of April 26.


=====================================================================
  THE PEOPLE
=====================================================================

  Anatoly Dyatlov   Deputy Chief Engineer. Present to direct the
                     test. Highest-ranking person in the control
                     room that night. Background in submarine
                     reactor engineering. The test had already been
                     delayed by two shifts.

  Aleksandr Akimov  Unit Shift Supervisor, Unit 4 night shift.
                     Responsible for operational decisions.

  Leonid Toptunov   Senior Reactor Control Engineer. Responsible
                     for control rod positions and reactor regimen.
                     25 years old, had worked independently for
                     approximately three months.

  Akimov and Toptunov both died of acute radiation syndrome in
  May 1986 after entering the destroyed reactor building to attempt
  to pump water into the ruined core. Akimov reportedly said on
  his deathbed that they had done everything right. Toptunov died
  three days later.


=====================================================================
  TIMELINE OF EVENTS
=====================================================================

  All times are local (Moscow Time, UTC+3).

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  APRIL 25, 1986
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  01:06   Scheduled shutdown begins. Gradual power reduction from
          3,200 MWt (100%) toward the test target of 700-1,000 MWt.

  03:47   Power reaches 1,600 MWt (50%). Reduction paused.

  14:00   ECCS (Emergency Core Cooling System) disconnected as part
          of the test procedure. The Kiev grid controller requests
          continued power output to meet evening demand. The test
          is delayed. The reactor holds at 1,600 MWt for 9 hours.

          During this hold, Iodine-135 is produced by fission and
          begins decaying into Xenon-135 — a powerful neutron
          poison (thermal absorption cross-section: 2.6 million
          barns). This is normal at steady power, but will become
          critical once power is reduced.

  23:10   Grid controller releases the reactor. Power reduction
          resumes. The day shift that was briefed on the test is
          long gone; the night shift takes over.

  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  APRIL 26, 1986
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  00:05   Power reaches 720 MWt. On track for the test.

  00:28   POWER COLLAPSES TO 30 MWt (~1%).

          When reactor control was switched from local to global
          automatic regulators, power plummeted. The exact cause
          is disputed:

            - INSAG-1 (1986): Operator error by Toptunov.
            - Dyatlov's account: Fault in the AR-2 automatic
              regulator system.
            - INSAG-7 (1992): Cause uncertain.

          Decision is made to continue the test and recover power.

          At 30 MWt, multiple effects reduce reactivity simultaneously:
          (1) Reduced power means less boiling — more liquid water
          in the channels absorbs more neutrons (void collapse).
          (2) Graphite moderator begins cooling, changing its
          moderation characteristics (temperature coefficient).
          (3) Xe-135 concentration begins rising as burnoff drops
          and I-135 from the 9-hour hold decays into fresh Xe-135.
          All three effects demand control rod withdrawal.

  ~00:32  Operators begin withdrawing control rods to recover power
  -01:00  against these combined reactivity losses. Multiple rod
          groups are pulled further than normal operating limits
          allow.

  01:00   Power stabilized at approximately 200 MWt (6% of rated
          power). The test was originally planned at 700-1,000 MWt.
          The operators decide to proceed at 200 MWt anyway.

          The Operating Reactivity Margin (ORM) is already violated.
          Standard operating rules require a minimum ORM equivalent
          to 15 control rods. Many rods have been withdrawn to
          overcome the xenon.

  01:03   Standby main circulation pump (MCP) connected to the
          left cooling loop.

  01:07   Additional MCP added to the right loop. The excessive
          coolant flow creates instabilities in the steam drum
          pressure and water level.

  ~01:19  Feedwater flow increased to compensate for dropping
          steam drum water levels. The operators block automatic
          trip signals from the steam drum separators to prevent
          an unplanned shutdown during the test. They are
          permitted to adjust the pressure setpoint; the water
          level setpoint is not actively changed but has been
          left at an incorrect value from prior operations.

  01:22   Steam drum level and pressure begin stabilizing, but the
  :30     extra cold feedwater suppresses steam production, which
          masks the true instability of the reactor.

          POST-ACCIDENT CALCULATIONS: ORM at this point equals
          only 6-8 manual control rods. Minimum required: 15.
          The reactor is operating in a regime never analyzed for
          safety by any independent body. (INSAG-7)

  01:23   TURBINE RUNDOWN TEST BEGINS.
  :04     Feed valves to Turbine Generator 8 are closed.
          The turbine begins coasting down. As it decelerates,
          it provides less and less power to the coolant pumps.
          Coolant flow through the core begins dropping.

  01:23   Coolant flow decreases. Steam begins forming in the
  :10-30  pressure channels. The positive void coefficient kicks
          in — as water turns to steam, neutron absorption drops,
          reactivity increases, power begins to rise.

  01:23   AZ-5 PRESSED — SCRAM INITIATED.
  :40
          Whether this was in response to slowly shifting parameters
          or as a routine end-of-test shutdown is unknown. There was
          no dramatic power surge before AZ-5 — the catastrophic
          excursion was triggered by the positive scram effect that
          followed. Both Akimov and Toptunov were dead within weeks.

          All 211 control rods begin inserting. But from their
          fully withdrawn positions, the graphite displacer tips
          enter the lower core first...

  01:23   POSITIVE SCRAM EFFECT.
  :40-43  The graphite tips displace the 1.25m water columns at
          the bottom of each rod channel. The lower core sees a
          sudden increase in moderation and a decrease in
          absorption — reactivity SPIKES.

  01:23   Power excursion alarms activate. Reactor output exceeds
  :43     530 MWt and is RISING. Fuel elements begin rupturing.

          The ruptured fuel increases steam generation further
          (positive void coefficient feedback).

          Synchro indicators in the control room show the rods
          at approximately 2-2.5 meters of their full 7-meter
          travel. Whether the rods physically jammed (from
          channel deformation) or were simply still in transit
          when the reactor was destroyed is unproven — the
          explosion occurred only 8-10 seconds after AZ-5, and
          simulations of the accident produce the same result
          regardless of whether the rods stop moving. Akimov
          disconnects the servo clutches to let the rods fall
          under gravity.

  01:23   PROMPT SUPERCRITICAL EXCURSION.
  :47     Reactor power reaches an estimated 30,000 MWt —
          approximately 10 times rated power, or 100 times the
          level it was operating at minutes before.

  01:23   First explosion (steam). The 1,000-tonne cover plate
  :58     is blown clear. A second explosion follows almost
          immediately — likely a steam explosion or (debated) a
          small nuclear excursion. The reactor core is destroyed
          and burning graphite is scattered across the site.


=====================================================================
  THE SIMULATION SCENARIO
=====================================================================

  The scenario file (scenarios/chernobyl.json) compresses the
  ~24-hour timeline into ~200 seconds of simulation time while
  preserving the key physics:

  Sim Time    Historical Event
  ────────    ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
  t=0-5       Reactor at ~50% power (1,600 MWt equivalent)
  t=5         23:10 — Power reduction resumes
  t=15        9-hour delay context. Xe-135 accumulating.
  t=30        00:28 — Power transferred to auto control
  t=32        Power collapses to ~1%. Deep rod insertion.
  t=45-47     Near-zero power. Xenon trap closing.
  t=60-70     Operators withdraw rods. Stabilize at 200 MWt.
  t=72        Auto control disabled. Manual operation.
  t=75-95     Progressive rod withdrawal past safety limits.
  t=95-97     01:19 — Extra feedwater, increased coolant flow.
  t=108       01:22:30 — ORM at 6-8 rods (minimum 15).
  t=120       01:23:04 — Turbine test begins.
  t=123-138   Coolant flow drops as turbine coasts down.
  t=150       01:23:30 — Power rising. Void coefficient active.
  t=160       01:23:40 — Akimov orders AZ-5.
  t=163       SCRAM. Graphite displacer tips enter core.
  t=172       Positive scram effect — reactivity spike.
  t=178       01:23:43 — Power exceeds 530 MWt.
  t=184       Rods at ~2-2.5m of 7m when reactor destroyed.
  t=190       01:23:47 — Prompt supercritical (~30,000 MWt).
  t=200       Reactor destroyed. Simulation pauses.

  What to watch for:

  - XENON BUILDUP (purple cells): After the power drop at t=32,
    watch Xe-135 cells accumulate. This is the "iodine pit."

  - ROD WITHDRAWAL (red/blue bars disappearing): From t=60-95,
    the operators progressively withdraw rods. The HUD shows
    rod insertion percentage dropping toward 0%.

  - STEAM FORMATION (black background cells): After t=120, cool
    water (dark blue) transitions to warm (dark red) to steam
    (black) as coolant flow drops.

  - THE SCRAM at t=163: Watch the control rods. They insert a
    few rows (blue graphite tips visible first!), then slow and
    stall as the power excursion deforms channels. In reality,
    synchro indicators showed rods at ~30% depth (~2-2.5m of
    7m) when the reactor was destroyed, though whether they
    physically jammed or were still moving is uncertain.

  - POWER SURGE: After the positive scram, the neutron count
    explodes. The HUD shows power climbing past 300%.


=====================================================================
  POINTS OF CONTENTION
=====================================================================

  The causes of the Chernobyl disaster remain debated. The two
  principal IAEA investigations reached opposite conclusions:

  INSAG-1 (August 1986)
  ─────────────────────
  Based on Soviet testimony presented in Vienna, led by nuclear
  scientist Valery Legasov. Conclusion: the accident was caused
  by "a remarkable range of human errors and violations of
  operating rules." The operators were primarily to blame.

  Six plant staff were tried and convicted. Dyatlov received 10
  years; Fomin and Bryukhanov 10 years each. The operators who
  died (Akimov, Toptunov, and others) were blamed posthumously.

  INSAG-7 (November 1992)
  ────────────────────────
  After the fall of the Soviet Union, new information became
  available including declassified KGB documents from 1971-1988.
  INSAG-7 reversed many of INSAG-1's conclusions:

  - "Most of the earlier accusations against staff for breach
    of regulations were acknowledged to be either erroneous or
    judged less relevant."

  - Turning off the ECCS was NOT a violation of regulations.

  - The positive scram effect (graphite tips) was the primary
    design cause. It had been known since 1983 but was never
    fixed.

  - "The accident was the result of the concurrence of specific
    physical characteristics of the reactor, specific design
    features of the reactor control elements, and the fact that
    the reactor was brought to a state not specified by
    procedures."

  However, INSAG-7 also stated: "INSAG remains of the opinion
  that critical actions of the operators were most ill judged."

  Dyatlov's defense
  ─────────────────
  After the accident, Dyatlov was sentenced to 10 years in prison.
  He maintained until his death in 1995 that the reactor designers
  and the officials who approved the flawed safety systems were
  the true culprits.

  Dyatlov's key argument: the operators were working within the
  procedures and manuals they were given. The positive scram effect
  (graphite displacer tips adding reactivity during emergency
  shutdown) was never documented in any operating manual. The
  operators had no way of knowing that pressing AZ-5 — the
  emergency shutdown button — could trigger an explosion. This
  design flaw was known to the reactor designers since 1983
  (Ignalina-1) but was classified and never communicated to
  plant staff.

  The test had been delayed by two shifts. The decision to
  continue after the power collapse was made based on the
  information available to the operators at the time.

  Modern consensus
  ────────────────
  Chernobyl was a systemic failure. The reactor had a fatal
  design flaw that its designers knew about and chose not to fix.
  Regulators failed to require changes. Operators were not
  informed of the risks — the positive scram effect was never
  in their manuals. INSAG-7 acknowledged that "most of the
  earlier accusations against staff for breach of regulations
  were either erroneous or judged less relevant." The ECCS
  disconnection, initially cited as a key violation, was found
  to be permitted under the procedures at the time.

  No single person or group caused Chernobyl. The disaster
  required failures at the design, regulatory, and institutional
  levels to align on the same night.


=====================================================================
  POST-ACCIDENT MODIFICATIONS TO RBMK REACTORS
=====================================================================

  Following Chernobyl, all remaining RBMK reactors received
  modifications to prevent a recurrence:

  - Control rods redesigned to eliminate the 1.25m water column
    below the graphite displacer, removing the positive scram.

  - A fast-acting emergency protection system (FAEP) installed:
    24 rods insert negative reactivity of at least 2-beta in
    under 2.5 seconds (vs. the original 18-second full insertion).

  - AZ-5 full insertion time reduced from 18 to 12 seconds.

  - Fuel enrichment increased from 2.0% to 2.4% to reduce the
    positive void coefficient. (Note: at the time of the accident,
    fuel burnup had reduced effective enrichment to ~1.3%, with
    fissile Pu-239/241 making up the balance. The lower delayed
    neutron fraction of plutonium (~0.21% vs. ~0.65% for U-235)
    made prompt supercriticality easier to reach.)

  - Additional absorbers added to the core to further reduce
    the void coefficient.

  - Operating procedures revised to require minimum ORM of 43-48
    manual control rods (increased from 15).

  As of 2026, multiple RBMK reactors in Russia remain operational
  under Rosenergoatom with repeated lifetime extensions. A 50-year
  lifetime was adopted for some units. Leningrad Unit 3 was
  extended to 2030 (July 2024), Leningrad Unit 4 to 2031
  (February 2026). Efforts are underway to extend all remaining
  units. All are operated by Rosatom's subsidiary Rosenergoatom.
